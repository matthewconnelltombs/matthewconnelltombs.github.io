The Brief

Geysers are fascinating natural wonders – the Old Faithful geyser in Yellowstone National Park being one of the most famous in the world. To me, one of the most beautiful things about Old Faithful is – you guessed it – the numbers!

Beyond its captivating eruptions lies a wealth of data that can be explored to uncover hidden patterns and relationships. In this analysis, we delve into the Old Faithful eruption dataset, using four distinct clustering techniques: K-Means, Ordering Points To Identify the Clustering Structure (OPTICS), Hierarchical Clustering, and Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM).

Our goal is to unravel the underlying structures behind the eruptions, shedding light on the dynamics of this geological phenomenon. Let’s start off by reviewing these clustering techniques:

  1. K-Means Clustering:
    • K-Means clustering is an algorithm for partitioning data into distinct groups, popular largely due to its simplicity.
    • This method focuses on identifying clusters based on distance to centroid points of clusters.

  2. OPTICS:
    • OPTICS focuses on identifying clusters based on data point density and neighboring points.
    • This method also detects noise/outliers in the data.

  3. Hierarchical Clustering:
    • Hierarchical clustering builds a tree-like structure of nested clusters called a dendrogram, providing a hierarchical view of the data.
    • Dendrogram analysis will reveal how closely related certain eruptions are, allowing clusters to be identified.

  4. Gaussian Mixture Models:
    • GMM is a statistical technique that assumes data is generated by a mixture of several probability distributions.
    • It models the underlying probabilistic distribution and forms clusters from a probabilistic perspective.
I have chosen to do this analysis in R using a R markdown file.

The Results

We can first visualize our data and make some predictions about what we might expect. It appears there are two main clusters, one centering around (2, 55) and the other around (4.3, 80). We can also see that there are a few points in between the two clusters – it is difficult to determine which cluster these belong to.

K-Means

When we turn to K-Means, we're entering the realm of simplicity and clarity. K-Means is a straightforward algorithm that partitions data into distinct groups based on their proximity to each other.

The algorithm does this by identifying central points in the data, called centroids, around which clusters form. The number of these clusters is determined by the user (k value). Although we believe that in this dataset, there should be two clusters, K-Means provides ways to check that. One of the ways we can check is the Elbow Method. We plot the total within sum of squares for a range of numbers of clusters, and look for an “elbow” in the line. Here we can clearly see this occurs at k=2, indicating there are two clusters.

As K-Means runs on the data, it assigns each eruption to the cluster whose centroid is closest. This process repeats until the algorithm converges, and we have well-defined clusters. We can see here K-Means is able to classify all but one point correctly (3.4, 65).

This emphasizes the importance of recognizing the limitations of K-Means in capturing intricate structures within the data. Despite this, K-Means remains a valuable tool for initial insights, offering a clear segmentation of eruptions and laying the foundation for further refinement in analysis.

OPTICS

As we navigate through the OPTICS algorithm, we find it steering away from the rigid cluster assignments of algorithms like K-Means. Instead, OPTICS explores the dataset, taking into account the varying density of data points. It begins by sorting points based on their reachability distances, a measure of how far each point is from its nearest neighbor.

The resulting reachability plot is a visual representation of the dataset's landscape, with peaks and valleys reflecting areas of high and low data density. These peaks become potential cluster centers, and the valleys define natural separations between clusters.

Unlike K-Means, where you define the number of clusters, in OPTICS you use an epsilon value, a cutoff value that establishes the maximum distance for a point to be considered part of a cluster. This parameter allows for a dynamic and adaptive clustering process, where the granularity of clusters can be adjusted based on the characteristics of the data.

One of OPTICS' strengths lies in its ability to handle clusters of varying shapes and sizes, making it particularly well-suited for this particular dataset. As we choose an epsilon, we not only identify clusters but also pinpoint outliers – data points beyond the reach of any cluster. This dual capability provides a nuanced understanding of the Old Faithful dataset, allowing us to capture both the regularity of clustered eruptions and the anomalies lying in isolation.

OPTICS appears to have done a better job clustering the points, with the algorithm deeming one point between the two clusters as noise. OPTICS faces challenges in accurately clustering data when the data clusters have varying densities.

HCLUST

Think of Hierarchical Clustering as the storyteller of our data. Instead of abruptly assigning data points to clusters, it weaves a tale of relationships, creating a dendrogram – a family tree of eruptions, if you will.

As we explore the Old Faithful eruption dataset through the lens of Hierarchical Clustering, the dendrogram unfolds, revealing connections and hierarchies. This visual narrative provides a nuanced understanding of the relationships between eruptions.

What makes the use of Hierarchical Clustering particularly insightful for our data is its ability to showcase nested clusters. Like discovering hidden chapters within a larger story, we can zoom in or out to explore overarching trends or pinpoint specific details. This hierarchical view captures not only the broader patterns of eruption similarities but also the subtle nuances that might be overlooked in a more rigid clustering approach.

While Hierarchical Clustering has many strengths, it also comes with a number of weaknesses. These include; being computationally expensive, having sensitivity to noise and outliers, lack of interpretability for large dendrograms.

Gaussian Mixture Models

Gaussian Mixture Models, or GMM, is a statistical method that takes a probabilistic stance in understanding data. Unlike some clustering techniques, GMM don't force data – in our case, eruptions – into predefined clusters. Instead, it assumes that the dataset is a mixture of several probability distributions.

In simple terms, GMM imagines that each eruption is generated by a unique blend of these distributions. It then iteratively refines its estimates of these distributions until it finds the best fit for the data.

The initial choice of probability distributions in GMM is crucial, as it not only influences the efficiency of the algorithm but also affects its susceptibility to getting stuck at suboptimal solutions during the iterative refinement process. We can see here that if a poor starting distribution is chosen, the algorithm can misclassify the clusters.

To get around selecting starting distributions, we can loop over random starting distributions and save whichever results in the best clustering (using log likelihood). This can help ensure we find the most optimal clustering.

GMM offers a unique perspective compared to other clustering techniques. Instead of clustering the data points into groups, GMM focuses on the probabilistic structures behind the data, allowing us to get a better understanding of not just the recorded eruptions, but how the eruptions happen.

The two biggest drawbacks about GMM is that we must make the assumption of a Gaussian distribution for clusters, and that GMM can often get caught in local optima.
